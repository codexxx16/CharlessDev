## The Question I Get Asked

"Why don't you just move to South Africa? Or the UK? Or the US?"

It's a fair question. The talent drain from Zimbabwe is real. Many of the best developers I know have left — for better salaries, better infrastructure, better opportunities. I understand why.

But I stayed. And I'm building here intentionally.

## The Advantages Nobody Talks About

### 1. You're Solving Real Problems

When you build software in a market with genuine friction, you're not building solutions looking for problems. You're solving problems that affect millions of people every day.

The financial inclusion gap in Zimbabwe is massive. Millions of people are unbanked or underbanked. Mobile money is the primary financial tool for most of the population. The infrastructure for digital commerce is still being built. That's not a disadvantage — that's a greenfield opportunity.

### 2. Lower Cost of Experimentation

My burn rate is a fraction of what it would be in London or New York. That means I can take more risks, run more experiments, and iterate faster without the pressure of burning through runway.

A failed experiment here costs me weeks. The same experiment in San Francisco might cost me months of runway.

### 3. Deep Market Understanding

I understand my users because I am my users. I use EcoCash. I experience load-shedding. I know what it's like to build on a 10Mbps connection (before Starlink). That empathy is worth more than any amount of user research.

## The Real Challenges

I won't pretend it's easy. The challenges are real:

**Infrastructure**: Power cuts (load-shedding) are a constant. I've built my workflow around them — battery backups, Starlink for internet independence, and a discipline of saving work constantly.

**Payment processing**: Getting paid internationally as a Zimbabwean developer is genuinely difficult. Most payment processors don't support Zimbabwe. I've had to get creative with Wise, Payoneer, and crypto.

**Isolation**: The local developer community is small. Most of my technical peers are online. Conferences, meetups, and in-person collaboration require travel.

**Perception**: Some international clients are hesitant to work with developers from Zimbabwe. I've learned to let the work speak for itself — and to be upfront about my location rather than obscuring it.

## What I've Built Here

Despite the challenges, I've shipped:

- A payment gateway handling thousands of transactions per month
- An AI-powered customer service platform for a local business
- A real-time inventory management system for a retail chain
- This portfolio site (obviously)

None of these required me to be in Silicon Valley. They required good internet (Starlink solved this), good tools (open source solved this), and good discipline (martial arts helped here).

## The Opportunity

Africa has 1.4 billion people. The median age is 19. Mobile internet penetration is growing rapidly. The problems to solve are enormous and the solutions being built elsewhere often don't fit the local context.

The developers who build for this market — who understand the constraints, the culture, and the opportunities — are going to build some of the most impactful software of the next decade.

I intend to be one of them.

## Practical Advice for Developers in Similar Situations

If you're a developer in a developing market wondering whether to stay or go:

1. **Build your online presence first** — GitHub, a portfolio, writing. Your work should speak for itself before your location becomes a factor.
2. **Specialize in your local market** — the knowledge you have about your market is genuinely valuable and hard to replicate.
3. **Solve the infrastructure problems** — Starlink changed everything for me. Don't let bad internet be your excuse.
4. **Build a global network** — Twitter/X, Discord communities, open source contributions. Your community doesn't have to be local.
5. **Charge global rates** — your location doesn't determine your value. Your skills do.

The world is remote now. Use it.
