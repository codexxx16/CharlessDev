## The Unexpected Connection

When people find out I train Muay Thai and Boxing alongside building software, they usually expect me to say something like "it helps me de-stress." That's true, but it misses the deeper connection.

Martial arts and software engineering are both **disciplines of iteration**. You don't become good at either through talent alone. You become good through thousands of repetitions, honest feedback loops, and the willingness to be a beginner again and again.

## Repetition Builds Intuition

In Muay Thai, you throw the same kick ten thousand times until it becomes automatic. Your body stops thinking and starts reacting. The technique is internalized.

In programming, the same principle applies. When I was learning React, I built the same todo app five different ways — with useState, useReducer, Zustand, Redux, and Jotai. Not because I needed five todo apps, but because repetition builds intuition. Now I can look at a state management problem and immediately feel which tool fits.

```typescript
// The first time you write this, you think carefully about every word
const [count, setCount] = useState(0)

// After the ten-thousandth time, your hands just type it
// The cognitive load is gone — you're thinking about the actual problem
```

## Embracing the Beginner's Mind

Every time I step into a gym with better fighters, I get humbled. I get hit. I make mistakes I thought I'd stopped making. And then I learn.

This is the **beginner's mind** — the ability to approach even familiar territory with openness and curiosity. It's what separates developers who plateau from those who keep growing.

When I started learning Rust after years of TypeScript, I felt like a beginner again. The borrow checker rejected code that felt perfectly reasonable to me. I was frustrated. But I kept the same mindset I bring to sparring: *what is this teaching me?*

```rust
// This doesn't compile — and Rust is right
fn get_first(v: &Vec<String>) -> &String {
    &v[0]
}

// You have to think about lifetimes
fn get_first<'a>(v: &'a Vec<String>) -> &'a String {
    &v[0]
}
```

The borrow checker wasn't being difficult. It was teaching me to think about memory ownership in a way I'd never had to before. That's a gift.

## Pressure Testing

In boxing, you can know every technique perfectly in training and still fall apart in sparring. The pressure of a real opponent exposes gaps you didn't know existed.

Code reviews and production incidents are the sparring sessions of software engineering. You can write beautiful code in isolation, but when it hits production — when real users find the edge cases, when the database is under load, when the network is unreliable — that's when you find out what you actually know.

I've learned to seek out pressure testing rather than avoid it:

- Deploy to production early, even when it's not perfect
- Ask for code reviews from people more experienced than me
- Write tests that simulate failure conditions, not just happy paths

## Recovery and Rest

One of the hardest lessons in martial arts is that rest is training. Overtraining leads to injury, burnout, and regression. The best athletes periodize their training — hard weeks followed by recovery weeks.

Developers burn out. I've done it. The signs are the same as overtraining: you stop enjoying the work, your output quality drops, you make careless mistakes.

Now I treat rest as part of the process. I take weekends off from personal projects. I go to the gym. I play games. I watch movies. And when I come back to the keyboard, I'm sharper.

## The Long Game

Martial arts is a lifelong practice. There's no finish line. A black belt isn't the end — it's the beginning of deeper study.

Software engineering is the same. There's always a new paradigm, a new language, a new problem domain. The developers I admire most aren't the ones who know the most — they're the ones who are still genuinely curious after twenty years.

Train hard. Rest well. Stay curious. The rest follows.
